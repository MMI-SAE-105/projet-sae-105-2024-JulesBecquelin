The Origins of Light: Domesticated Fire

Introduction

The history of human lighting dates back hundreds of thousands of years, with the mastery of fire by early humans being essential for their survival. In *The Domestication of Fire in Paleolithic Times*, archaeologist Henry de Lumley explores how this discovery transformed Paleolithic societies.

The Discovery of Fire

The discovery of fire marks a decisive turning point for early humans. Before this discovery, the night was a realm of darkness, dangers, and uncertainties. With fire, Paleolithic humans not only gained a source of heat and protection but also the ability to extend their activities beyond sunset.

The First Evidence of Controlled Fires

According to Henry de Lumley, the first tangible evidence of fire domestication dates back to around 400,000 years ago. Archaeological excavations have revealed hearths in Africa, Europe, and Asia where fire was regularly used. These hearths were carefully organized, showing that humans had learned to control and maintain flames to illuminate their surroundings.

Uses of Fire

Fire was not only used for lighting. It allowed for cooking food, providing warmth during harsh winters, and keeping predators at bay. However, its role in lighting was fundamental. For the first time, humans could push back the limits of darkness. Campsites could be illuminated, fostering nocturnal social life and the development of early rituals around the flames.

Fire as a Source of Light

The light from fire was unlike any other natural source. It created an atmosphere conducive to exchange and communication. The dancing shadows cast by the flames on cave walls likely played a role in the development of parietal art and the symbolic imagination of early humans.

Cultural and Cognitive Transformations

According to Henry de Lumley, the domestication of fire also transformed human cognitive abilities. By enabling more active night life, fire encouraged the emergence of new forms of social cooperation, task sharing, and knowledge transmission. Fire likely also stimulated the development of language, as it provided opportunities for exchange and discussion around the hearths.

The Evolution of Lighting Methods

Fire remains the first lighting technology, but over time, human societies perfected its use. Torches, grease lamps, and later oil lamps were developed, expanding lighting possibilities. This refinement would continue for millennia until the invention of modern lighting systems.
